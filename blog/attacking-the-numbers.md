"Accountants are dead inside," I declared quietly for the second time. I had never taken the course wanting to be an accountant, nor did I ever plan to. But while accounting felt mechanical, mathematics classes held my interest—specifically, the friction point where human cognition meets technological execution.

To illustrate this friction, consider a recent conversation I had with an AI language model, 

**Claude:** Right. So total cost is 500 + 240 = ?
**Me:** 740.
**Claude:** Careful, 500 + 240 is not 740. Try that addition again.
**Me:** Okay, what's the answer?
**Claude:** I'm not going to give it to you—you can do this addition yourself.

Out of context, the AI's math error is secondary to its pedagogical stance. By refusing to spoon-feed an answer, it forced a pause. Critical thinking, much like meaningful mathematics, requires exactly this: a deliberate disruption of passive execution to look closely at the underlying process. Mathematics is a high-level language designed to frame formal, logical constructs. Yet, in practice, the classroom environment often betrays this purpose.

My primary observation during my recent mathematics module class was that the level of cognitive inference—among lecturers and students alike—was remarkably low. Very little is expected of students, and perhaps even less is demanded of educators. Mathematics and business teachers frequently project a distinct prickliness, a dissatisfaction born from dealing with a mentally taxing, slow-moving subject while trying to artificially force student speed.

This obsession with speed misconstrues how the mathematical mind actually operates. To move past "critical thinking" as a modern buzzword, we must transition away from passive absorption (memorizing procedural algorithms) toward autonomous, purposeful judgment.

In his foundational work, Thinking, Fast and Slow, Daniel Kahneman unpacks the dual systems of human cognition. Consider the problem $17 times 24$. If asked for an immediate, calculator-free guess, a mathematically fluent mind pauses. You instinctively know the range cannot be in the thousands because $20 times 25 = 500$, and both original numbers are lower than these bounds. If you guess $300$, your internal logic reminds you that $15 times 20 = 300$, which is too low. A sophisticated guess lands around $400$. The actual answer is $408.

Unless one is a savant, a two-digit multiplication problem forces an abrupt shift from System 1 (fast, intuitive, effortless) to System 2 (slow, deliberate, analytical). Popular culture expects people who are "good with numbers" to think fast on their feet. In reality, the true strength of mathematical thinking lies in the slow, agonizing practice of System 2 processing. If cognitive constructs are best retained when framed in a language we understand, then mathematics is the ultimate language for testing validity. To build this capacity, students require a nurturing system that values structural depth over algorithmic speed. Before we can enter the "gym" of hard, college-level business mathematics, we must first examine the framework of how we think about the numbers themselves. 

To build this capacity students need to learn to listen to their inner critical voices when it comes to mathematics and deal with any biases that they have. Students (as well as teachers) also need to be open to the idea of learning math in a nurturing system that values structural depth over rote learning, or algorithmic speed.
